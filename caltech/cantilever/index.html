Cantilever Beam Analysis

The second solids simulation I conducted was a beam-bending problem. First I worked out the problem analytically given the boundary conditions (bending and torsion) then ran a similar simulation in ANSYS. The simulation resulted in a slightly larger displacement and significantly lower twist angle, possibly due to the fact that the analytical solution did not consider the endcap used in the simulation geometry. Next, a modal analysis was performed in ANSYS revealing three mode frequencies characterized by strong z-axis bending, strong y-axis bending, and torsion respectively (characteristic shapes for these last two modes shown below). Given that the beam loaded under the provided boundary condition appears to be characterized by torsion and y-axis bending, it is likely that these two modes would be excited if the boundary conditions were suddenly removed and the system allowed to vibrate freely.

For the experimental section, we learned to use strain gages by conducting a cantilever beam test to verify Poisson’s ratio and elastic modulus for aluminum.

The cantilever beam test was quite straightforward so two variants were conducted: load controlled, where weights were incrementally added to the edge of the beam, and displacement controlled where a micrometer was used to deflect the beam. The strain gage data was converted in MATLAB using the strain transformation equations and plotted against the loads. These relationships were analyzed with Euler-Bernoulli beam theory, yielding an estimate for the elastic modulus of 68.7 GPa, very close to the tabulated value of 69 GPa for aluminum. All this data in turn could be used to compute Poisson’s ratio. The average value found between the trials was 0.306, fairly close to the tabulated value of 0.33.
