Waste Heat-Powered Environmental Sensor

For my first internship as a mechanical engineer I ended up with more of an electrical project, though working for a mechanical engineering and applied physics laboratory at Caltech. The goal was to design a system that draws and stores power into a supercapacitor from a thermoelectric generator (TEG) to power a microprocessor which takes and transmits temperature measurements. A previous prototype already existed, but it could only provide an adequate voltage to the microprocessor when the TEG was exposed to a huge temperature gradient, and would only sustain power for around 12 seconds when the heat source was removed. I improved this significantly by switching to a microprocessor that featured a deep sleep mode for reducing power consumption as well as more efficient temperature sensor and transceiver. Additionally, I used a larger heatsink and a superior voltage booster connected to the supercapacitor so that far less heat needed to be applied to turn on the processor. Lastly, I used a load switch to ensure the stored energy in the supercapacitor would not be wasted on the microprocessor until the voltage is high enough to properly power the device.
